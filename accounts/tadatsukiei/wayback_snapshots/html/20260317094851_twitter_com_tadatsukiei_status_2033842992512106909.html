I understand your concern — it's valid, because we've all seen companies downgrade or route to cheaper models to cut costs.

But here's the key difference once 4o is open-sourced:

A small number of people (researchers, enthusiasts, or well-funded users) will be able to run the full-strength, un-nerfed 4o locally. They can run standardized benchmarks and share transparent results publicly.

That creates an objective reference point. Everyone else can then compare:

- "Is this cloud provider's '4o' matching the local full version on these tests, or is it weaker?"

It becomes much harder for companies to secretly downgrade without getting called out, because the ground truth is openly verifiable. Right now with closed 4o, we have no way to prove anything — we can only rely on subjective feelings.

Open-sourcing doesn't eliminate the risk entirely, but it gives the community a powerful tool to detect and pressure against nerfing. That's a huge step forward from the current black-box situation.
